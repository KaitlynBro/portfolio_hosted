Instacart

Overview

Instacart is an American company that has branched out to Canadian stores, providing a grocery delivery and pick-up service.

This service allows customers to order groceries and other various products (depending on your geographical location and what stores are offered in that area) from participating retailers. The shopping is done by a personal shopper, then delivered to your door, or packed away at the store to await your pickup.

Background

When I decided to do a UX case study on Instacart, my intent was to learn if individuals find the flow of research - select - buy to work as expected, and in turn, uncover how/if this flow can be improved upon.

I created a Research Report outlining my methodology, participant requirements, and how I would calculate or determine my findings.

I chose to use remote, unmoderated usability testing, and the System Usability Scale to analyze my results.

I conducted my usability testing on 6 participants - 3 who had used the app before, and 3 who had not, and had them complete a series of task-based instructions.

Understanding the problem

After reviewing each participants answers, as well as conducting a Heuristic Analysis on the app myself, I discovered 3 reoccurring pain points.

  • Individuals were frustrated by having to click the back arrow multiple times to return to a main page, as opposed to a standard single click Home button

  • Individuals did not like that there isn't an easy way to undo an undesired action such as deleting an item from the cart by mistake. This means the user would have to manually find the item again and place it back in the cart, as opposed to simply clicking "undo"

  • There is little transparency regarding checkout fees until the very end of your shopping journey.

Proposed solution

I chose to focus on the checkout fees pain point - I felt I could make the most impact by improving this flow. My main goals while working on this flow were:

  • I wanted users to have significant information available to them immediately into their shopping journey
  • I wanted full transparency around checkout fees
  • I wanted users to be confident in the Instacart platform to improve customer retention

Usability testing results

I conducted a second round of usability testing; this time, taking them through a series of task basked instructions while they interacted with my prototype. My participants were chosen at random, in a remote, moderated session, each lasting about 13 minutes in length.

My findings from the second round of testing are outlined below:

  • 100% of users were confident in what fees they were paying for prior to reaching the final checkout page due to having that information accessible on a storeâ€™s landing page
  • 100% of users were surprised at the placement of the Checkout Fees and More Info button on the home page, and felt it looked more like simple information rather than an actual link

In summary, both users appreciated learning about checkout fees early in their shopping journey, but may not have found this important information on their own, with the flow I had created

Key Takeaways & Recommendations

My second round of testing uncovered two key points.

First, having important information like checkout fees directly on a stores landing page, as opposed to only on the last page of the shopping journey, improved customer trust and confidence in shopping on Instacart by 100%. My participants agreed that they felt much more knowedgeable and comfortable with what fees they were paying by having the ability to access a breakdown of that information so early into their shopping.

However, my second key finding was that I failed to provide the most effective way for a customer to find this important information. I chose to place a link to checkout fees directly under a store's logo under More Info, but both participants felt that it did not 'feel' like a clickable link, and initially thought the link was simply general - unclickable - information.

If I were to move forward on this study, my next step would be to consider these options:

  • Return to the drawing board and find a better location within the store's landing page to place a link for checkout fee information
  • Place a tooltip that would show up on a first visit, indicating to a user that they can find more information by clicking on that link
  • Add an interative guided tour where I would point out the link to the user on their first visit

In summary, I found many pieces of important and interesting information while conducting the entirety of this study. I do believe that I found an effective resolution to a known pain point that would be worth it to implement on the condition that further testing be conducted to find the best spot to place a link to checkout fees.
